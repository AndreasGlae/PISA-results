Influence of Country, Gender, Class Size, and Immigration Status¶

on Math and Reading performances of secondary school children¶

by Andreas Gläsle¶

Summary¶

Investigation Overview¶

Many factors can influence school performance of children. I wanted to find out which of them do obviously correlate with school grades. Some especially interesting factors are shown here, and the way they correlate with the main course performances of secondary school children, according to the results of the PISA study of 2012.

Dataset Overview¶

The dataset contains the results of the PISA study in 2012. PISA discovers the specific conditions of pupils' learning in most countries of the world and creates a ranking of the best nations in an overall view. The database has 636 columns and about 485,000 rows of students of countries all over the world.

Summary of findings¶

In the Exploration Part, we found out from what countries the participants came, how's the Grade and Gender distribution and some facts about the parents' schooling and job status. Looking at the Bivariational Analysis, we found out that the education level 3B, 3C of mothers is correlated with lower grades than the other education levels, but couldn't prove that result for only german participants in a violin plot, which suggested that low education levels of the mother clearly go along with lower grade performance of the student. We found interesting differences in the class sizes of six selected countries, showing that Colombia has bigger classes than the other states that we looked at. In the Multivariate Exploration, we first looked at the correlation between class size, gender and math grade levels. Then we tried to find out how the relation between reading and math grade level is. The result was that there seems to be a correlation, but the possible combinations are in a wide variety, so there seems to be room for specialists in reading on one side or in math on the other side.

Key Insights for presentation¶

Visualization 1: Reading Performance is correlated to the Immigration Status of pupils¶

I wanted to investigate the Reading Performance compared to the Immigration Status, for german pupils as a Violin plot.

I selected the german pupils out of the database and looked at their reading performance, categorized by their immigration status, depending on if they are native inhabitants or siblings of immigrants in the second or in the first generation. This can be visualized quite well by a Violin plot.

We can see that the median of reading performance is over 530 for native Germans (with half of the results between 470 and 600 points), but only about 500 for second generation immigrants and about 450 for first generation immigrants who are rather new in the country. This is a very consistent result meaning the longer a person is in the country, the better is its German language knowledge (not really surprising, though). Still, for all three immigration status categories there's a wide range of results of the reading performance, so there should be other factors influencing the reading performance, too.

Visualization 2: The Math and Reading Performance correlate to Class Sizes and Grade¶

Here we look at the data of six selected countries (Germany, Italy, France, Poland, the USA, and Colombia), shown in a PairGrid.

From this chart we can see that the best scores in Math performance can be seen in classes between 10 and 40 pupils, that Math performance correlates with higher grades. The same applies to Reading performance, but the range of scores isn't as wide here as in Math performance.

Visualization 3: Gender doesn't influence Math performance, but class size does in an unexpected way. The range of Math grade levels differs between the countries.¶

I wanted to investigate the Math Performance compared to the Class sizes and Gender, for pupils of each of six selected countries of Europe and North and South America, in a Facet Grid.

Apparently, there is little effect of gender (in Colombia, the male side seems to have slightly higher grades). It looks like class size has an influence on Math grades, and the best class sizes seem to be between about 20 and 35, which would be a remarkable result because I would have expected a more linear relationship showing that smaller classes lead to better grades.

The highest Math grade levels of above 750 can be seen mainly in the European countries, whereas the lowest come from Columbia and Italy. The class sizes in Colombia reach much higher amounts than in the European countries where class sizes are limited to amounts below 40.
